Where I come from

Born in Dublin, study brought me to London in 2010 for a degree in Japanese and Social Anthropology at SOAS. Since then, I've served ramen in Tokyo, trained at the European Commission in Brussels, and returned to London to work in news media.

What i do now

Right now I work as an assistant correspondent at the UK bureau of The Yomiuri Shimbun, Japan's largest daily newspaper. There, I help the team of correspondents stay abreast of UK and European news - researching stories on politics, economics and sports.

I'm kept busy working to tight print deadlines across timezones to deliver news to the people in Japan. Working all the way through Brexit and trying to explain it to an audience on the other side of the world was, until recently, some of the most difficult work I've done - but then I tried RegEx!

Why programming?

My road to programming

I've been curious about programming for a long time, but never felt it was something I could do - I didn't see myself as particularly mathematical, and I believed that meant the doors were closed to me.

But after hearing about Founders & Coders through an alumnus of the course, I attended an information session in July 2017 and began learning Javascript, CSS and HTML.

the programming I want to do

People-centric

I want the things I make to feel accessible. I want to demystify programming and technology in the same the way it is opening up to me now.

Working with individuals and on small projects will probably mean working with people who feel like I did - that programming is an opaque, esoteric process.

I want to be able to take these people on the development journey with me, and leave them with a functional understanding of the process.

Empowering

with areas like fintech and healthtec booming, i can really see the possibility of using technology to help people.

I love the idea of using tech to put power back in the hands of people - as citizens, consumers and users.

Having myself benefited from things like personal finance apps and price comparison websites, I would love to be able to use what I learn to help others.

Why founders & coders?

A great ethos

Paying it forward

I love the idea of passing on knowledge that was given to you; helping other people as you were helped yourself.

With alumni teaching students, and its strong ties with non-profits, Founders and Coders' pro-social, cooperative approach has an immediate appeal for me.

Inclusivity and Community

I get excited about inclusivity in all spheres of life and work - when you open up areas seen as unwelcoming or inaccessible to some, everybody wins.

Having attended the info sessions and meetups, I know that Founders & Coders not only fosters a durable community among its students and alumni, but also provides a welcoming space for people of all backgrounds, and for people who may not have thought the tech industry would welcome them.

A great route into tech

Practical approach

I know from having spoken to current students and alumni that Founders and Coders is high-intensity, practical learning.

no financial barriers

When I decided to learn to code, I knew I wanted to do it face-to-face. I'm a social learner, and the ability to talk things out with others is something I value hugely. But at between eight and ten thousand pounds, programming courses are a huge investment.

Founders & Coders being tuition-free would give me an opportunity to move into tech without taking on massive debt.
